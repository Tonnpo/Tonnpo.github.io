Given a nonempty set \\(\Omega \\), a \\(\sigma\\)-algebra of subsets of \\(\Omega\\) (denoted by \\(\mathcal{F}\\)), and two equivalent probability measures \\(\mathbb{P}\\) and \\(\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}\\) (i.e. they agree on which sets in \\(\mathcal{F}\\) have probability zero), the **Radon-Nikodym derivative**, \\(Z\\), of \\(\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}\\) with respect to \\(\mathbb{P}\\) is an almost surely positive random variable with \\(\mathbb{E} Z = 1\\) and

$$
Z = \frac{d \widetilde{\mathbb{P}}}{d \mathbb{P}}
$$


Now, an important example is for a probability space \\( (\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})\\), let \\(X\\) be a standard normal random variable and define

$$
Z(\omega) = \exp \{ - \theta X(\omega) - \frac{1}{2} \theta^2 \}
$$

for a constant \\(\theta\\) and for all \\(\omega \in \Omega \\). We can check that \\(Z\\) is almost surely positive for all \\(\omega \in \Omega\\) and \\(\mathbb{E}Z = 1 \\). Therefore, we can use the random variable \\(Z\\) to introduce an equivalent probability measure \\(\\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}\\). This probability measure is commonly referred to as the **risk-neutral probability measure**. We can extend this idea from a random variable to a stochastic process.

For an almost surely positive random variable \\(Z\\) with \\(\mathbb{E}Z = 1\\) on a probability space \\((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})\\) and a filtration \\(\mathcal{F}(t)\\), define the **Radon-Nikodym derivative process** 

$$
Z(t) = \mathbb{E} [Z \vert \mathcal{F}(t)]
$$

The **Girsanov theorem** states that for a Brownian motion \\(W(t)\\), \\(0 \leq t \leq T\\), on a probability space \\((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})\\) and a filtration, \\(\mathcal{F}(t)\\), generated by this Brownian motion, let \\(\Theta (t)\\), \\(0 \leq t \leq T\\), be an adapted process and define


$$
Z(t) = \exp \{-\int_0^t \Theta(u) dW(u) - \frac{1}{2} \int_0^t \Theta^2(u) du\}
$$

$$
\widetilde{W}(t) = W(t) +\int_0^t \Theta(u) du
$$

with

$$
\mathbb{E} \int_0^T \Theta^2 (u) Z^2 (u) du < \infty
$$

Then, let \\(Z = Z(T)\\) and use it to introduce an equivalent probability measure \\(\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}\\). We have that \\(\widetilde{W}(t)\\) is a Brownian motion under \\(\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}\\). To see why this result is important, consider a discounted stock price process

$$
D(t)S(t) = S(0) \exp \{\int_0^t \sigma(s) dW(s) + \int_0^t (\alpha(s)- R(s) - \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2(s))ds \}
$$

Or equivalently,

$$
d(D(t)S(t)) = (\alpha(t) - R(t))D(t)S(t) dt + \sigma(t)D(t)S(t)dW(t)
$$


Depending on the processes \\(\alpha(t)\\) and \\(R(t)\\), \\(D(t)S(t)\\) may or may not be a martingale with respect to the Brownian motion \\(W(t)\\). We hope that, by a change of measure, we can turn \\(D(t)S(t)\\) into a martingale with respect to a Brownian motion under a different probability measure. In order to do that, we choose an adapted process

$$
\Theta (t) = \frac{\alpha(t) - R(t)}{\sigma(t)}
$$

Then, we use the Girsanov theorem and obtain a Brownian motion under the new probability measure \\(\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}\\)

$$
\widetilde{W}(t) = W(t) +\int_0^t \Theta(u) du
$$

Or equivalently,

$$
d\widetilde{W}(t) = dW(t) + \Theta(t) dt
$$


This implies that


$$
\begin{align*}
d(D(t)S(t)) &= (\alpha(t) - R(t))D(t)S(t) dt + \sigma(t)D(t)S(t)dW(t) \\
&= \sigma(t)D(t)S(t)(\Theta(t) dt + dW(t)) \\
&= \sigma(t)D(t)S(t)d\widetilde{W}(t) 
\end{align*}
$$

And so under \\(\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}\\), a discounted stock price process is a martingale.
